What the Bodhisattva Vow Assumes

An exploration of where Buddhist claims fall on the line between direct experience and faith.

The Vow

The vow is easy to say. In its traditional form, something like: may I attain full awakening not for myself but for the liberation of all sentient beings — in this and every future lifetime, until not one remains.

As an ethical aspiration, it's genuinely striking. It takes the compassion that contemplative practice seems to cultivate and turns it into a structural commitment. Not just: I will be kinder. But: I orient my entire existence — across however many lifetimes it takes — toward others. The Bodhisattva ideal, in Mahayana Buddhism, is the practitioner who could exit the cycle of rebirth entirely but chooses to remain, returning again and again, until every sentient being in every realm is liberated.

I encountered this recently and found myself wanting to take it seriously — not dismiss it, not treat it as decoration. Doing that, though, meant sitting with what the vow actually assumes.

· · ·

An Inspectability Test

There's a useful test for any claim: can you look and check?

The non-dual insight — the thing sitting practice points at — passes this test cleanly. When a teacher says the sense of being a separate self is constructed, that thoughts arise without a thinker, that there's no central agent behind your experience — you can actually look. The territory is accessible to direct observation. The result, if the claim is right, is the same regardless of what you believed before you sat down. Someone who has never heard of Buddhism can sit down, watch their experience closely, and find out whether the claim holds up.

This is what makes the empirical core of Buddhism unusual among religious frameworks. Not that it's infallible. Not that it's easy to see. But that it's checkable. The same logic applies to impermanence, to the relationship between craving and suffering, to the constructed nature of the self. These are observations, not doctrines. You can verify them through direct investigation rather than accepting them on authority.

The question I kept returning to: which parts of the Bodhisattva vow pass this test, and which don't?

The Spectrum

Not all Buddhist claims are the same kind of thing. Some can be verified in a single sitting. Others require extraordinary meditative development. Others require prior cosmological beliefs that the practice itself never validates. Here's a rough map.

Click any claim to expand it.

Directly inspectable Requires faith
Suffering from craving Inspectable
Notice craving arise — toward food, validation, relief. Notice the quality of experience that accompanies it. You can verify this in a single sitting without any background belief. The claim is: craving generates a particular kind of suffering, and releasing craving generates relief. This is available to anyone who looks.
Impermanence Inspectable
Every phenomenon that arises passes. The sound, the sensation, the thought, the mood — none of it stays. You can watch this directly. No cosmology required. The only thing you need is a reliable method for observing your own experience closely enough to notice.
No-self Inspectable
The claim that the sense of being a unified, continuous self is a construction — not a fact about the territory but an appearance in experience. You can look for the self and not find it. This is what the practice is largely pointing at. The territory is available; the method is direct investigation.
Compassion from practice Plausible
The claim that as practice deepens, a natural orientation toward the wellbeing of others emerges — not as an ethical stance imposed from outside but as something that arises on its own. Widely reported, and plausible in the sense that something like this does seem to happen. It's harder to verify than impermanence because it unfolds over years, not sittings — but it's the kind of claim that could, in principle, be tested.
Karma within a lifetime Plausible
In its most secular form: actions have consequences that shape future experience. Habitual patterns create grooves. The quality of your attention and intention shapes outcomes over time. This is broadly compatible with secular psychology and doesn't require any prior cosmological commitment.
Rebirth Requires faith
The claim that consciousness — or something like it — continues beyond the death of the body and arises again in a new form. This is not checkable through ordinary first-person observation. It occupies a genuinely ambiguous middle ground: neither confirmed nor falsified, not obviously magical, but not analogous to impermanence either. No feedback loop exists — you can't update the belief through direct experience.
Karma across lifetimes Requires faith
Once you accept rebirth, the claim that karma carries across those lifetimes follows. But it inherits all the epistemic problems of rebirth and adds more: you'd need not just continuity of something across lives but a mechanism of transfer and a way for present-life experience to reflect past-life causes. None of this is accessible to practice or investigation in any ordinary sense.
The Bodhisattva Vow Requires faith
To attain full awakening not for oneself but for the liberation of all sentient beings — across all future lifetimes, until every being in every realm is free. It presupposes: rebirth (yours and others'), karma across those lives, sentient beings in multiple realms, and a timeline measured in eons. None of this can be verified through practice. You're holding an intention toward a cosmological outcome whose premises can't be inspected — and that's worth knowing, even if it doesn't change whether you take the vow.

The claims on the left share a property: you can verify them without background beliefs, without decades of practice, and without any particular faith commitment. The claims on the right require something different. You're not extending direct observation. You're accepting a framework.

Where the Vow Falls

The Bodhisattva vow doesn't just assume rebirth — it requires a specific cosmological architecture. Multiple realms, beings across those realms, karma that accumulates and transfers across lives, a timeline measured in eons. The "all sentient beings" in the vow includes, in the traditional formulation, beings in hell realms, hungry ghost realms, animal realms, god realms, and human realms across infinite cycles of time.

None of this can be verified in a sitting. Or through years of practice. Or through any method currently available to practitioners at any level of development. There's no feedback loop — no way to update these beliefs through direct observation. You accept them on the basis of teaching and tradition.

This is worth naming clearly, not as a criticism but as a category distinction. The inspectable claims and the cosmological claims are structurally different kinds of things. One set you can test; the other you inherit. Both exist inside the same tradition, and from the outside they can be easy to conflate.

The tradition itself is aware of the tension. The Diamond Sutra's resolution is that the vow dissolves the very self that would accomplish it — to liberate all beings is to see through the illusion of a self that liberates and beings that are liberated. The vow is performative, not literal. It's a beautiful move, and it may be right. But it quietly evacuates most of the vow's cosmological content, which is worth noticing if you're reciting the full traditional form.

· · ·

Two Ways to Hold It

Practitioners who find the inspectable core of Buddhism genuinely compelling end up in an interesting position when they encounter the vow. At least two honest responses seem available.

The first: hold the vow as an instrument rather than a metaphysical claim. The commitment to orient toward the liberation of others, to not treat practice as a private project — that's real and livable without the cosmological frame. And it actually works. Orienting attention toward others reduces self-referential processing. Committing to practice on behalf of others rather than just yourself shapes behavior in ways that are themselves inspectable. What remains when you strip the cosmology is something like: I aspire to practice in a way that benefits others, not just myself. That's a genuine ethical stance, and its effects can be felt without infinite lifetimes or beings in other realms.

The second: take the metaphysics seriously, and accept that you're operating on faith. This is coherent too. Religious traditions have always asked practitioners to hold some claims on faith while verifying others through experience. The question is whether you're doing this knowingly — whether you've made a conscious decision, rather than simply inherited an assumption without examining it.

The position worth examining is the middle one: reciting the full vow with its traditional cosmological weight while privately treating it as "just metaphor," without having thought through what that means. The vow can bear either reading. The thing to avoid is holding both simultaneously without noticing.

· · ·

The inspectable core of Buddhism — the parts that survive the test — is genuinely unusual. The claim that the self is constructed, that suffering tracks craving, that impermanence runs through everything: these are available to anyone who looks carefully enough. That's worth holding onto.

The Bodhisattva vow sits elsewhere. It's built on the tradition's most expansive vision of what compassion could become — and on a cosmological framework that, taken literally, requires the same kind of faith as any other religious cosmology. That's not a dismissal. It's an observation about what kind of thing the vow is. Knowing where it sits on the map doesn't make it less beautiful. If anything, it makes the commitment more deliberate.

If you've taken the vow — or have thought carefully about how you hold the cosmology — I'd be interested in your perspective. The question feels worth staying with.
