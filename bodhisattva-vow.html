What the Bodhisattva Vow Assumes

An exploration of where Buddhist claims fall on the line between direct experience and faith.

The Vow

The vow is easy to say. In its traditional form, something like: may I attain full awakening not for myself but for the liberation of all sentient beings — in this and every future lifetime, until not one remains.

As an ethical aspiration, it's genuinely striking. It takes the compassion that contemplative practice seems to cultivate and turns it into a structural commitment. Not just: I will be kinder. But: I orient my entire existence — across however many lifetimes it takes — toward others. The Bodhisattva ideal, in Mahayana Buddhism, is the practitioner who could exit the cycle of rebirth entirely but chooses to remain, returning again and again, until every sentient being in every realm is liberated.

I encountered this recently and found myself wanting to take it seriously. The problem is that taking it seriously requires noticing what the vow assumes.

· · ·

An Inspectability Test

There's a useful test for any claim: can you look and check?

The non-dual insight — the thing sitting practice points at — passes this test cleanly. When a teacher says the sense of being a separate self is constructed, that thoughts arise without a thinker, that there's no central agent behind your experience — you can actually look. The territory is accessible to direct observation. The result, if the claim is right, is the same regardless of what you believed before you sat down. Someone who has never heard of Buddhism can sit down, watch their experience closely, and find out whether the claim holds up.

This is what makes the empirical core of Buddhism unusual among religious frameworks. Not that it's infallible. Not that it's easy to see. But that it's checkable. The same logic applies to impermanence, to the relationship between craving and suffering, to the constructed nature of the self. These are observations, not doctrines. You can verify them through direct investigation rather than accepting them on authority.

The question I kept returning to with the Bodhisattva vow is whether any part of it passes this test.

The Spectrum

Not all Buddhist claims are the same kind of thing. Some can be verified in a single sitting. Others require extraordinary meditative development. Others require prior cosmological beliefs that the practice itself never validates. Here's a rough map.

Directly inspectable Requires faith
Suffering from craving Notice craving arise — toward food, validation, relief. Notice the quality of experience that accompanies it. You can verify this in a single sitting without any background belief. The claim is: craving generates a particular kind of suffering, and releasing craving generates relief. This is available to anyone who looks. Directly inspectable
Impermanence Every phenomenon that arises passes. The sound, the sensation, the thought, the mood — none of it stays. You can watch this directly. No cosmology required. The only thing you need is a reliable method for observing your own experience closely enough to notice. Directly inspectable
No-self The claim that the sense of being a unified, continuous self is a construction — not a fact about the territory but an appearance in experience. You can look for the self and not find it. This is what the practice is largely pointing at. The territory is available; the method is direct investigation. Directly inspectable
Compassion arises from practice The claim that as practice deepens, a natural orientation toward the wellbeing of others emerges — not as an ethical stance imposed from outside but as something that arises on its own. This is widely reported and plausible. It's harder to verify than impermanence because it unfolds over years, not sittings. But it's the kind of claim that could be tested in principle. Plausible, not yet verified
Karma within a lifetime In its most secular form: actions have consequences that shape future experience. Habitual patterns create grooves. The quality of your attention and intention shapes outcomes over time. This is broadly compatible with secular psychology and doesn't require any prior cosmological commitment. Whether "karma" is the right word for it is a different question. Broadly compatible with secular view
Rebirth The claim that consciousness — or something like it — continues beyond the death of the body and arises again in a new form. This is not checkable through ordinary first-person observation. In principle it makes empirical predictions (about what survives, what transfers), but no method currently available can test them. It occupies an ambiguous middle ground: neither confirmed nor falsified, not obviously magical but not analogous to impermanence either. Requires prior belief
Karma across lifetimes Once you accept rebirth, the claim that karma — accumulated through action, intention, and understanding — carries across those lifetimes follows. But it inherits all the epistemic problems of rebirth and adds more: you'd need not just continuity of something across lives but a mechanism of transfer and a way for present-life experience to reflect past-life causes. None of this is accessible to practice or investigation in any ordinary sense. Requires prior belief
The Bodhisattva Vow To attain full awakening not for oneself but for the liberation of all sentient beings — across all future lifetimes, until every being in every realm is free. This is the most ambitious commitment in any religious tradition. It presupposes: rebirth (yours and others'), karma across those lives, sentient beings in multiple realms (humans, animals, and traditionally, hungry ghosts, hell-beings, gods), and a timeline measured in eons. None of this can be verified through practice. You're holding an intention toward a cosmological outcome whose premises can't be inspected. Full cosmological framework

The claims on the left share a property: you can verify them without background beliefs, without decades of practice, and without any particular faith commitment. The claims on the right require something different. You're not extending direct observation. You're accepting a framework.

Where the Vow Falls

The Bodhisattva vow doesn't just assume rebirth — it requires a specific cosmological architecture. Multiple realms, beings across those realms, karma that accumulates and transfers across lives, a timeline measured in eons. The "all sentient beings" in the vow includes, in the traditional formulation, beings in hell realms, hungry ghost realms, animal realms, god realms, and human realms across infinite cycles of time.

None of this can be verified in a sitting. Or through years of practice. Or through any method currently available to practitioners at any level of development.

This isn't unique to Buddhism. It's the standard structure of religious metaphysics: claims about what happens after death, about unseen beings and realms, about cosmic-scale processes that operate beyond ordinary perception. Believing in rebirth, karma across lifetimes, and liberation across infinite realms requires approximately the same epistemic posture as believing in resurrection or divine judgment. The content differs. The architecture is the same — you're accepting claims about a world you can't directly access, based on teaching and tradition.

There's something genuinely interesting here. The tradition is aware of the paradox. The Diamond Sutra's resolution is that the vow dissolves the very self that would accomplish it — to liberate all beings is to see through the illusion of a self that liberates and beings that are liberated. The vow is performative, not literal. But if that's the resolution, it quietly evacuates most of the vow's content, including the cosmology that made it feel like such a serious commitment in the first place.

Two Ways to Hold It

Practitioners who find the inspectable core of Buddhism genuinely compelling end up in an odd position when they encounter the vow. Two honest responses seem available.

The first: hold the vow as motivation, not metaphysics. The commitment to orient toward the liberation of others, to not treat practice as a private project — that's real and livable without the cosmological frame. What remains is something like: I aspire to practice in a way that benefits others, not just myself. That's a genuine ethical stance. It doesn't require infinite lifetimes or beings in other realms to be meaningful.

The second: take the metaphysics seriously, and accept that you're operating on faith. This is coherent. Religious traditions have always asked practitioners to hold some claims on faith while verifying others through experience. The question is whether you're doing this knowingly — whether you've made a decision, rather than simply inherited an assumption.

The position to watch out for is the middle one: chanting the full vow with its traditional cosmological weight, while privately treating it as "just metaphor," without examining whether that assumption costs anything. The vow can bear either reading. What it can't do is be both simultaneously.

· · ·

The inspectable core of Buddhism — the parts that survive the test — is genuinely unusual. The claim that the self is constructed, that suffering tracks craving, that impermanence runs through everything: these are available to anyone who looks carefully enough. That's worth something.

The Bodhisattva vow is a different kind of thing. Built on the tradition's most expansive vision of what compassion could mean, and on a cosmological framework that, taken literally, asks exactly what any other religion asks: to believe, on faith, in a world you can't directly access. Knowing that doesn't make the vow less beautiful. It makes it more honest to hold.
